Buy Me a Coffee

Currently in Italy there are more inmates than what the system is designed to host, althoguh the situation is probably worse. In fact, the official regular capacity of the country's detention system - which currenty stands at places - does not account of missing spots.

Despite many OpenData initiatives, the Ministry of Justice provides data regarding inmate population in a format that is not machine readable. Every month, associations working to protect the rights of people deprived of liberty - such as the nonprofit organization Antigone - are forced into tedious data collection work, often manual, which leaves a lot of room for errors and consumes many resources and time.

To address this issue, I began a project aimed at collecting and processing the data made available by the Ministry in a way that make it more useful to those who need them. Every number included in the current page is automatically updated every 15th of the month, when a scraper is set up to visit the Ministry website, collect and process the data. Code and data collected so can be accessed on GitHub.


Prison overcrowding, where do we stand compared to the regulatory capacity

At the beginning of 2020, the irruption of COVID-19 in Italy convinced the authorities to lower presences in detention centers to slow the contagion and reduce risks for the imprisoned population. Despite that initiative, even during the high of the pandemic the amount of people in detention centers was above the official regular capacity.

Since the pandemic stopped being an emergency, detention centers around the country went back to increasing the hosted population of inmates. Soon, Italy might reach the record levels reached in 2010, when Italy - which at that time had about one million more residents - hosted almost 68,000 inmates.

On average, those numbers mean that across all of 189 Italy's detention cetners there are inmates for every regular spot. However, this situation is probably worse.


Regular Capacity is not Real Capacity
